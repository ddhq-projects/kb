Scrumban is a hybrid agile methodology that combines Scrum's structured iteration planning with Kanban's continuous flow and visual workflow management. It enables teams to balance predictability with flexibility, making it ideal for projects with shifting priorities and evolving requirements.

## Core Principles

- **Workflow visualization** ensures every work stage is visible on a board, helping teams identify bottlenecks.
- **Work-in-progress limits** cap how much work can be in each stage simultaneously, preventing team overload and context switching.
- **Flow-based management** introduces continuous flow principles within iterations.
- **Explicit process policies** communicate workflow requirements to all team members.

## Key Practices

**Creating a Scrumban board** — an evolved Scrum board that captures the complete workflow including stages like "Design/Analysis," "Development," "Ready for review," and "Testing." Provides deeper visibility into where work items slow down.

**Imposing WIP limits** on each column prevents bottlenecks. When a limit is reached, team members help peers finish current work instead of starting new items.

**Flexible planning** — teams plan only when needed (when WIP falls below a predefined threshold), saving time spent on recurring planning meetings.

**Flow-based metrics** replace velocity measurements with cycle time and throughput. Relies on actual delivery data rather than story point estimations.

## Advantages and Disadvantages

| Aspect | Advantage | Disadvantage |
|--------|-----------|--------------|
| Flexibility | Adapts to changes mid-project | Reduced predictability; risk of scope creep |
| Efficiency | WIP limits improve focus; no recurring estimation | More complex to implement |
| Roles | Empowers teams with less micromanagement | Lack of clear role definitions can create confusion |
| Metrics | Uses actual flow data instead of estimates | Loss of velocity tracking makes forecasting difficult |
