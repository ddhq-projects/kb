# ITIL 4 — Detailed Overview

ITIL 4 is a flexible framework for IT service management (ITSM) that emphasizes value co-creation, agility, and integration with modern practices like Agile, Lean, and DevOps. Released in 2019, it remains widely used into 2026, though ITIL Version 5 launched earlier in 2026 as an evolution for AI-driven digital product and service management.

## Service Value System (SVS)

The SVS is the central model of ITIL 4. It shows how all components and activities of the organization work together to enable value creation. The SVS includes:

- **Service Value Chain (SVC)** — six flexible, non-linear activities
- **Guiding Principles** — seven recommendations for decision-making
- **Governance** — the organization's direction and control
- **Service Management Practices** — 34 practices organized in three categories
- **Continual Improvement** — applied to all SVS components
- **Four Dimensions** — holistic view of service delivery

External inputs: demand and value. External outputs: products and services.

## Service Value Chain — 6 Activities

### 1. Plan
Establishes a shared understanding of the organization's vision, current status, and improvement direction across all products and services and all four dimensions.

**Inputs:** Policies, requirements, constraints
**Outputs:** Strategic, tactical, operational plans; Portfolio decisions; Architecture documents; Policies; Improvement opportunities

**Key activities:**
- Define the vision, mission, and strategy
- Ensure portfolio alignment with strategy
- Define policies and standards
- Ensure governance is in place

### 2. Improve
Ensures continual improvement of products, services, practices, and all four dimensions.

**Inputs:** Product/service performance; Stakeholder feedback; Improvement opportunities; Third-party requirements
**Outputs:** Improvement plans and status reports; Updated improvement register

**Key activities:**
- Review and analyze improvement opportunities
- Prioritize and plan improvements
- Implement improvements
- Verify and measure improvement results

### 3. Engage
Provides a good understanding of stakeholder needs, transparency, and continual engagement and good relationships with all stakeholders.

**Inputs:** Product/service portfolio; High-level demand; Formal requests; Feedback
**Outputs:** Consolidated demands; Requirements; User support tasks; SLAs/XLAs; Performance reports; Project documentation

**Key activities:**
- Foster relationships with stakeholders
- Capture and prioritize demands
- Negotiate and agree on SLAs
- Provide transparency through reporting

### 4. Design & Transition
Ensures that products and services continually meet stakeholder expectations for quality, costs, and time to market.

**Inputs:** Portfolio decisions; Architectures; Policies; Requirements; Constraints
**Outputs:** Requirements and specifications; Service and product components; Contract and agreement requirements; Improvement opportunities

**Key activities:**
- Design services and products
- Architect and design requirements
- Review and improve designs
- Transition products and services

### 5. Obtain/Build
Ensures that service components are available when and where they are needed, and meet agreed specifications.

**Inputs:** Architectures; Policies; Contracts; Agreements; Service components; Improvement opportunities
**Outputs:** Service components; Contracts and agreements; Requirements and specifications; Performance information; Improvement opportunities

**Key activities:**
- Obtain from external/internal suppliers
- Build and assemble components
- Verify that components meet specifications

### 6. Deliver & Support
Ensures that services are delivered and supported according to agreed specifications and stakeholders' expectations.

**Inputs:** New/changed products and services; Service requests; Improvement opportunities
**Outputs:** Services delivered; Support tasks completed; Information on the completion of user support tasks; Performance information; Improvement opportunities

**Key activities:**
- Deliver services to agreed levels
- Resolve incidents and fulfill service requests
- Support users
- Manage operational access

## Four Dimensions of Service Management

1. **Organizations & People** — organizational structure, culture, roles, competencies, staffing levels, location
2. **Information & Technology** — information, systems, technology, tools, security, architecture
3. **Partners & Suppliers** — relationships with other organizations involved in design, development, deployment, delivery, support, improvement
4. **Value Streams & Processes** — how activities are organized and connected to deliver value

## Seven Guiding Principles

1. **Focus on value** — everything the organization does should generate value for stakeholders
2. **Start where you are** — don't start from scratch; assess current state objectively
3. **Progress iteratively with feedback** — resist the temptation to do everything at once
4. **Collaborate and promote visibility** — work together across boundaries, make work visible
5. **Think and work holistically** — no service, practice, or department stands alone
6. **Keep it simple and practical** — use minimum necessary steps; if a process is too complex, simplify
7. **Optimize and automate** — maximize value of work by carrying it out efficiently; automate where appropriate

## 34 Management Practices

### General Management Practices (14)

| Practice | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| Architecture management | Explains how organizational elements and their interrelations enable value creation. Defines structural aspects of the organization. |
| Continual improvement | Aligns practices and services with changing business needs through ongoing enhancements. Uses continual improvement model. |
| Information security management | Protects information by managing risks to confidentiality, integrity, and availability. |
| Knowledge management | Maintains and improves effective, efficient, and convenient use of information and knowledge across the organization. |
| Measurement and reporting | Supports fact-based decision-making by collecting, analyzing, validating, and storing relevant data. |
| Organizational change management | Ensures smooth implementation of changes by focusing on human aspects, communication, and adoption. |
| Portfolio management | Ensures the organization has the right mix of programs, projects, products, and services to execute strategy. |
| Project management | Plans and manages temporary endeavors to deliver products, services, or results. |
| Relationship management | Nurtures links between the organization and its stakeholders at strategic and tactical levels. |
| Risk management | Identifies, assesses, and treats risks to ensure sustainability and value creation. Integral to all activities. |
| Service financial management | Supports organization's strategies and plans for service management by ensuring effective use of financial resources. |
| Strategy management | Formulates goals and ensures they are achieved through the allocation of appropriate resources. |
| Supplier management | Manages suppliers to ensure quality provision and collaborative value realization. |
| Workforce and talent management | Ensures the organization has the right people with the appropriate skills and competencies in correct roles. |

### Service Management Practices (17)

| Practice | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| Availability management | Ensures services deliver agreed levels of availability to meet business needs. |
| Business analysis | Analyzes business needs and recommends solutions that deliver value to stakeholders. |
| Capacity and performance management | Ensures services meet agreed performance and capacity levels cost-effectively and predictably. |
| Change enablement | Maximizes the number of successful service changes by ensuring risks are assessed, changes are authorized, and deployment is coordinated. |
| Incident management | Minimizes the negative impact of incidents by restoring normal service operation as quickly as possible. |
| IT asset management | Plans and manages the full lifecycle of IT assets to maximize value, control costs, manage risks, support decision-making, and meet regulatory requirements. |
| Monitoring and event management | Observes services and service components, filters and categorizes events, and takes appropriate action. |
| Problem management | Reduces the likelihood and impact of incidents by identifying root causes and managing workarounds and known errors. |
| Release management | Makes new and changed services and features available for use. |
| Service catalogue management | Provides a single source of consistent information on all operational services and those being prepared for operation. |
| Service configuration management | Ensures accurate and reliable information about the configuration of services and service assets is available when needed. |
| Service continuity management | Ensures the availability and performance of services at an acceptable level during and after a disaster. |
| Service design | Designs new or changed services to be fit for purpose and fit for use. |
| Service desk | Captures demand for incident reports and service requests from users. Single point of contact. |
| Service level management | Ensures services deliver agreed levels through a constant cycle of agreeing, monitoring, reporting, and reviewing. |
| Service request management | Supports agreed service requests as part of normal service delivery. |
| Service validation and testing | Ensures that new or changed products and services meet defined requirements. |

### Technical Management Practices (3)

| Practice | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| Deployment management | Moves new or changed hardware, software, documentation, or other components to live or test environments. |
| Infrastructure and platform management | Oversees the infrastructure and platforms used to deliver services. Includes IT operations. |
| Software development and management | Ensures applications meet internal and external stakeholder requirements for functionality, security, and compliance. |

## ITIL 4 vs Earlier Versions

| Aspect | ITIL v3/ITIL 2011 | ITIL 4 |
|--------|-------------------|--------|
| Structure | Service lifecycle (Strategy, Design, Transition, Operation, CSI) | Service Value System + Value Chain |
| Focus | Process-centric | Value-centric, co-creation |
| Practices | 26 processes | 34 practices (flexible, not rigid) |
| Integration | Standalone ITSM | Integrates with Agile, DevOps, Lean |
| Terminology | Process, function | Practice, value stream |
| Certification | Foundation → Expert | Foundation → Managing Professional → Strategist |

## ITIL Version 5 (2026)

ITIL v5 launched in 2026 as an evolution focused on AI-driven digital product and service management. It builds on ITIL 4's foundations with emphasis on AI, automation, and digital transformation.
